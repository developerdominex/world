Socrates

Socrates (469–399 BCE) was a classical Greek philosopher credited as a founder of Western philosophy. He focused on ethics and human behavior through dialogue and questioning, known as the Socratic Method.

"The unexamined life is not worth living." – Socrates

He believed that knowledge and virtue were closely connected and that self-awareness was the path to wisdom.

Plato

Plato (427–347 BCE), a student of Socrates, founded the Academy in Athens. He wrote philosophical dialogues exploring justice, politics, and reality.

"Wise men speak because they have something to say; fools because they have to say something." – Plato

Plato's works introduced concepts like the Theory of Forms, describing ideal versions of reality, influencing philosophy, politics, and education for centuries.

Aristotle

Aristotle (384–322 BCE), a student of Plato, focused on logic, ethics, science, and politics. His writings form the foundation of many modern disciplines.

"Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom." – Aristotle

Aristotle emphasized empirical observation and categorization, and his concept of virtue ethics remains influential today.

Alexander the Great

Alexander III of Macedon (356–323 BCE), known as Alexander the Great, was influenced by Greek philosophy, particularly Aristotle, who was his tutor. Alexander combined military genius with cultural vision, spreading Hellenistic culture across three continents.

"There is nothing impossible to him who will try." – Alexander the Great

Example Stories

Socrates and the Oracle: Socrates visited the Oracle of Delphi, who proclaimed him the wisest man. He questioned this by examining others’ wisdom, realizing his own knowledge was limited. This story highlights his commitment to questioning and understanding.
Plato's Allegory of the Cave: Prisoners in a cave see only shadows and believe them to be reality. One escapes and discovers the real world, symbolizing enlightenment and the pursuit of knowledge.
Aristotle's Lyceum: Aristotle founded a school in Athens, where he taught students through observation and logic, emphasizing learning through experience.
Alexander and the Gordian Knot: Alexander solved the seemingly impossible knot by slicing it with his sword, demonstrating bold thinking and action.

Legacy

Greek philosophy shaped Western thought, education, and science. The works of Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle continue to influence ethics, politics, and logic. Alexander’s conquests spread Greek culture, art, and ideas across the known world, creating a lasting Hellenistic legacy.

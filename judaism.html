Judaism

Judaism is one of the oldest monotheistic religions, centered on the belief in one God and the covenant between God and the Jewish people.

History

Judaism traces its origins over 3,000 years ago in the ancient Near East. Its foundational figure is Abraham, regarded as the first patriarch, who entered into a covenant with God. Moses is another central figure, traditionally credited with leading the Israelites out of Egypt and receiving the Torah, the sacred law. Over centuries, Jewish communities spread across the world, maintaining religious and cultural identity despite periods of persecution and diaspora.

Core Beliefs

Judaism emphasizes belief in a single, all-powerful God, ethical conduct, and the importance of community. Key beliefs include the covenant between God and the Jewish people, the observance of the Torah, and the expectation of a future messianic era.

  • Monotheism – Belief in one God who is just, merciful, and involved in the world.
  • The Torah – The sacred text containing laws, commandments, and teachings.
  • The covenant – Special relationship between God and the Jewish people.
  • Importance of ethics, justice, and compassion in daily life.

Practices

Jewish religious life involves prayer, study, and observance of commandments (mitzvot). Practices include:

  • Observing the Sabbath (Shabbat) from Friday evening to Saturday evening as a day of rest and worship.
  • Celebrating holidays such as Passover, Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Hanukkah.
  • Daily prayer and reading from the Torah.
  • Kashrut – Dietary laws regulating permitted foods and preparation.

Branches of Judaism

Judaism has several major movements, reflecting different approaches to religious law and modern life:

  • Orthodox Judaism: Strict adherence to Jewish law and tradition.
  • Conservative Judaism: Balances traditional observance with modern adaptation.
  • Reform Judaism: Emphasizes personal interpretation of scripture and modern values.
  • Reconstructionist Judaism: Views Judaism as an evolving civilization with cultural and religious significance.

Influence

Judaism has deeply influenced world culture, ethics, and religion. It provided the foundation for Christianity and Islam, shaped Western legal and moral systems, and contributed to literature, philosophy, science, and art. Jewish communities worldwide continue to practice their faith, preserve traditions, and contribute to global culture and society.
